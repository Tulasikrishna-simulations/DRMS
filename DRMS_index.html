01 — The Problem

Three structural failures. Decades of papers. No fix.

The global monetary system has three problems. Economists have known about them for a long time. What they've mostly done is write papers describing the problems in careful detail and attend conferences to discuss those papers. I'm 18 and from Hospete and I decided to just try to fix it.

Problem 01

Single-Reserve Dependency

The US dollar is the global reserve currency. When the Federal Reserve raises rates because American inflation is inconvenient, every developing economy holding dollar-denominated debt gets crushed simultaneously. They didn't vote for this. They didn't cause the inflation. They simply owe money in a currency they don't print.

Problem 02

Structural Imbalances

Nations are locked into dollar-denominated debt cycles that constrain domestic fiscal policy entirely independent of their own economic performance. A country that wants to spend on infrastructure must first make sure its dollar creditors are calm. Its own needs come second.

Problem 03

Geopolitical Contamination

The Russian ruble didn't collapse in 2022 because Russia suddenly became economically incompetent. It collapsed because geopolitical decisions were transmitted through financial infrastructure. Currency values reflected political anger, not economic reality. That's a design flaw, not a feature.

03 — The Mathematics

Three models. One honest derivation.

DRMS formalises currency strength as a state variable C(t) governed by an ODE whose inputs are four measurable economic indicators. Three models were developed — each fixing a specific failure in the previous one. The reasoning for every choice is below.

Model 1 Linear Foundation Discarded
$$\frac{dC}{dt} = k_1 \cdot T(t) \cdot G(t) \;-\; k_2 \cdot I(t) \cdot D(t)$$

Trust and growth drive appreciation. Inflation and instability drive depreciation. T·G are multiplied — not added — because growth without trust doesn't compound. A country can post 10% GDP growth while its institutions are collapsing and no one will believe it continues.

The problem: C(t) is unbounded. Positive performance → currency grows forever. Negative performance → crosses zero, goes negative. A negative currency strength is not an economic insight. It's a number with no meaning.

Model 2 Proportional Growth Discarded
$$\frac{dC}{dt} = C(t)\cdot\left[k_1 T(t) G(t) \;-\; k_2 I(t) D(t)\right]$$

Growth is now proportional to current currency strength — the same structure as population growth models. A stronger economy sees larger absolute changes from the same performance signal.

The problem: exponential growth without a ceiling. For viable economies, C(t) → ∞. No currency should appreciate forever. One more fix needed.

Model 3 Stability-Constrained Primary Model
$$\frac{dC}{dt} = C(t)\cdot\left[k_1 T G - k_2 I D\right] \;-\; \lambda\,[C(t)]^2$$

The $-\lambda C^2$ term introduces quadratic resistance that caps currency at a finite equilibrium. Why $n=2$? Linear resistance ($n=1$) gives no finite equilibrium. Cubic ($n\geq3$) is excessively aggressive. Quadratic is the minimal nonlinearity that produces a finite stable fixed point. This is a Bernoulli ODE of order $n=2$ — exact closed form solution exists.

The honest note: $\lambda$ was initially treated as a constant. In v4 it becomes $\lambda(C) = \lambda_0 + \lambda_1 C$ — dynamic damping that increases with scale. Which means $C^* = \alpha/\lambda$ is the v1-v3 equilibrium. The v4 equilibrium requires solving a quadratic: $\lambda_1 C^{*2} + \lambda_0 C^* - \alpha = 0$.

The Bernoulli Derivation — Full Working

Defining $\alpha = k_1 TG - k_2 ID$ (net performance signal), the substitution $u = 1/C(t)$ transforms the nonlinear ODE into a linear one. Every step is shown — because skipping steps is how mistakes survive.

01

Substitution

$$u(t) = \frac{1}{C(t)} \implies \frac{du}{dt} = -\frac{1}{C^2}\frac{dC}{dt}$$
02

Substitute dC/dt into du/dt

$$\frac{du}{dt} = -\frac{1}{C^2}(\alpha C - \lambda C^2) = -\frac{\alpha}{C} + \lambda = -\alpha u + \lambda$$
03

Standard Linear Form

$$\frac{du}{dt} + \alpha u = \lambda$$
04

Integrating Factor — the sign matters

$$\mu(t) = e^{+\alpha t} \quad \leftarrow \text{positive exponent}$$

The original derivation used $e^{-\alpha t}$. That was wrong. The final answer survived because the errors in numerator and denominator cancelled each other out. The intermediate step was still incorrect — and in any formal submission, that gets marked wrong regardless of whether the answer happens to be right.

05

Integrate Both Sides

$$\frac{d}{dt}\!\left[u\cdot e^{\alpha t}\right] = \lambda e^{\alpha t} \implies u\cdot e^{\alpha t} = \frac{\lambda}{\alpha}e^{\alpha t} + K$$
06

Apply Initial Condition $u(0) = 1/C_0$

$$K = \frac{1}{C_0} - \frac{\lambda}{\alpha}$$
07

Closed-Form Solution — exact for constant $\alpha$

$$\boxed{C(t) = \frac{1}{\;\dfrac{\lambda}{\alpha} + \!\left(\dfrac{1}{C_0} - \dfrac{\lambda}{\alpha}\right)\!e^{-\alpha t}\;}}$$

As $t\to\infty$ with $\alpha>0$: the exponential vanishes and $C(t)\to\alpha/\lambda = C^*$. The global attractor.
Important: this derivation assumes $\alpha$ is constant. In the real model all four inputs are time-varying, so $\alpha=\alpha(t)$ and no closed form exists. The numerical simulation handles this correctly by recomputing $\alpha$ at every timestep. The closed form is an exact solution for the constant-parameter approximation only.

Viability Condition — Necessary and Sufficient

$$k_1\cdot T(t)\cdot G(t) \;>\; k_2\cdot I(t)\cdot D(t)$$

When this fails, the only stable equilibrium is $C=0$. Every trajectory collapses regardless of initial conditions. $C^* = \alpha/\lambda$ only exists and is physical when $\alpha>0$. Negative $C^*$ values that appear when $\alpha<0$ are mathematical artefacts outside the model's domain — not economic predictions. They should never appear in a results table. They did in Version 1. That was fixed.

DRMS v4 — Master Equation

$$\frac{dC_i}{dt} = C_i\!\left(k_1(t)\,T_i\,G_{\text{eff},i} - k_2(t)\,I_i\,D_i + k_3 M_i\right) - \lambda(C_i)\,C_i^2 + \beta R_i(t) + \sum_j \gamma_{ij}(t)(C_j-C_i)C_i + \sigma_i C_i\,dW_t + J_i\,dN_t$$
$C_i(k_1(t)\,T_i G_{\text{eff}} - k_2(t)\,I_i D_i)$
Domestic performance signal. Trust × normalised growth drives appreciation. Inflation × instability drives depreciation. k₁(t) and k₂(t) are time-varying — fitted from historical data, not hand-picked.
$+ k_3 M_i$
Market sentiment. Exchange rates aren't purely macroeconomic — speculation and psychology move them too. M captures what the fundamentals don't.
$- \lambda(C_i)\,C_i^2$
Dynamic stability cap. λ(C) = λ₀ + λ₁C — resistance increases with scale. Large economies face stronger stabilising forces. This changes the equilibrium from α/λ to the solution of a quadratic.
$+ \beta R_i(t)$
Recovery injection. IMF support, debt restructuring, structural reform. Countries do come back — the model needs to allow for that. Below C_critical this term determines whether recovery is possible.
$+ \sum_j \gamma_{ij}(t)(C_j - C_i)C_i$
International coupling — asymmetric and time-varying. Trade flows, financial exposure, supply chains. When Ukraine happened, γᵢⱼ(t) changed across the network. Sanctions restructure the graph.
$+ \sigma_i C_i\,dW_t + J_i\,dN_t$
Stochastic shocks plus jump diffusion. dWₜ models continuous noise. J·dNₜ (Poisson process) models sudden crashes — the kind Brownian motion alone can't represent. Found in a Cengage calculus book at 2am.

06 — The Audit

Twelve errors. All documented. All fixed.

Most researchers quietly fix mistakes and hope nobody notices. I did the opposite — built an entire structured section around every error I found, ranked by severity, corrected in full. None of them invalidate the framework. The mathematics is just considerably cleaner now.

#9
Critical

Corrections Report Used k₁ = k₂ = 35

A prior corrections report assumed k₁=k₂=35 and rebuilt the entire 18-country table on this. Back-calculation from the original portfolio: Germany gives k₁=40.0, k₂=30.0. India confirms. All four countries confirm. The report corrected the wrong numbers. Bangladesh was misclassified as non-viable as a result. With correct k₁=40, k₂=30: α=0.192 → C*=0.384 → VIABLE.

#1
High

Integrating Factor Sign Error

Wrote e^(−αt) for the integrating factor. Correct is e^(+αt). The final C(t) expression survived because the sign errors in numerator and denominator cancelled. The derivation was still wrong at the intermediate step — and that would be marked incorrect in any formal submission regardless of the final answer.

#10
Medium

Bifurcation Threshold ~4% — Correct Is ~16.1%

Stated critical inflation rate of ~4% for India's parameters. Correct calculation: I_crit = k₁TG/(k₂D) = (40×0.65×0.065)/(30×0.35) = 1.69/10.5 ≈ 16.1%. The bifurcation diagram in the same document visually showed 0.15–0.20. The diagram and the calculation agreed with each other. The text stood alone at 4%. This is not a rounding error.

#5
Medium

Negative C* Reported as a Meaningful Result

Argentina received C*=−35.5 in the results table. C(t)≥0 by domain restriction. Negative equilibrium values are artefacts of evaluating α/λ when α<0 — outside the model's valid domain. For non-viable economies, C*(economic)=0. A negative number in a results table is not a finding.

#6
Medium

Argentina Inflation: 50% in Code, 80% in PDF

Two different numbers in two documents about the same thing. Neither is correct. IMF IFS data 2015–2023: 27%, 41%, 26%, 48%, 54%, 42%, 49%, 95%, 211% — average 66%. Corrected to I=0.660. k₂·I·D = 30×0.660×0.75 = 14.85. Qualitative conclusion unchanged.

#11
Medium

Constant-α Closed Form Presented for Time-Varying System

α(t) was defined as explicitly time-varying. The closed-form solution was presented as though α is constant. When α=α(t) no simple closed form exists — the integrating factor becomes e^(∫α(t)dt). Correct framing: closed form is exact for constant α only. Numerical integration handles the general case.

#2
High

Missing Stability Linearisation Algebra

Jumped from f′(C)=α−2λC directly to f′(C*)=−α without showing the substitution. At C*=α/λ: f′(C*)=α−2λ(α/λ)=α−2α=−α. The factor of 2 from differentiating C² was skipped. A reader cannot reproduce the derivation without this step.

#4
Medium

C₀=0 Declared Unconditionally Unstable

Stability table stated C=0 is UNSTABLE without any condition on α. But f′(0)=α — positive when α>0 (unstable) and negative when α<0 (stable). Argentina and Nigeria converging to zero in the simulation directly contradicts the table I wrote.

#7
Medium

Euler Δt=1.0 Near Stability Boundary

JavaScript used Δt=1.0. Stability requires Δt<2/α. For China (α=1.315): Δt_max≈1.52 — dangerously close. Instability was handled by clamping to [−300, 300]. That's the computational equivalent of solving an overflow by declaring numbers cannot exceed 300.

#3
High

Notation: c vs C, Missing Time Arguments

Handwritten notes alternated between c(t) and C(t) for the same variable. Case is semantics in mathematics, not style. T, G, I, D written without time arguments implied constants. λ·C(t)² written without brackets could be parsed as λ·C·t².

#12
Medium

Silent Parameter Changes in Corrected Table

The corrections report changed Germany's growth from 2.5% to 1.5%, India's trust from 0.65 to 0.62 — no source cited. A corrected table must document all parameter changes. Otherwise it's impossible to know whether differences in C* are due to corrected formulas or just different inputs.

#8
Low

integral() Silently Clamps Negative C

JavaScript integral function used Math.max(0, d.C) — silently returning near-zero for non-viable economies, distorting variance analysis. Fix the solver so C≥0 is enforced at integration level. Then the clamp is unnecessary.

I'm telling you this not as an excuse for the gaps but as context for why finding them matters. Nobody handed me the list of things to check. I made the list myself. And if you find mistakes I've missed, I would be extremely grateful for any feedback.

The Identifiability Problem

k₁ and k₂ always appear as products — k₁TG and k₂ID. Which means if trust and growth tend to move together across time — and they often do, because countries with good governance tend to also have stable growth — the data genuinely cannot always tell k₁ apart from T. They're doing the same job in the equation. I noticed this after building the fitting objective. This isn't a bug I introduced. It's a fundamental problem that affects every structural macroeconomic model ever written. What solving it requires: instrumental variables, cross-country panel variation, or structural constraints from theory. I don't have those yet. But I know exactly what I'm missing.

The Meese-Rogoff Benchmark

In 1983, Meese and Rogoff proved that no structural exchange rate model beats a random walk at short horizons. That finding has held for over 40 years. So if someone asks whether DRMS beats a random walk at one-year prediction — the honest answer is probably not, and that's not surprising. Anyone telling you their structural model does is either lying or evaluating on in-sample data. What DRMS is actually claiming is different and more modest: that structural fundamentals determine long-run equilibrium regimes — viable or collapse. The 18-country table is preliminary evidence for that claim. That's the right evaluation horizon for this kind of model.

Econometric Rigor

The current fitting gives point estimates for k₁, k₂, λ. It doesn't give confidence intervals. It doesn't test stationarity of the input series before fitting. It doesn't verify causal structure — DRMS assumes T, G, I, D cause C, but without instrumental variables that claim is correlation dressed as causation. I found each of these gaps by name after building the thing they apply to. Confidence intervals are fixable via bootstrap. Stationarity tests are one function call. Causal identification is genuinely hard and I'm not pretending otherwise.

Mathematical Completeness

Existence and uniqueness for the base Model 3 ODE: provable by Picard-Lindelöf since the right-hand side is locally Lipschitz for C>0. Lyapunov stability for the deterministic case: V(C)=(C−C*)² is a candidate — dV/dt<0 near C* can be shown. Stochastic stability for the full SDE: requires Itô's lemma, can be sketched. Full rigour for the endogenous coupled system where T, I, G, D are themselves governed by ODEs: not proven yet. I know what the proof would require. I'm not there yet.

Currency Modeling Scope

Real FX systems involve derivatives markets, central bank intervention, speculative flows, and geopolitical reserve dynamics. DRMS models structural macroeconomic equilibrium — it captures the fundamental long-run drivers, not the daily noise. This is a deliberate scope decision, not an oversight. The question DRMS answers is: given these structural fundamentals, where does the equilibrium lie? Not: what will the exchange rate be tomorrow? Those are different questions and confusing them is how you misread the model.

Where In Hospete

There's no economist in Hospete to ask. There's no library with the relevant journals. There's no supervisor to catch the identifiability problem before I built three weeks of fitting on top of it. Every gap above I found the same way: reading something at 2am, recognising that a word I just learned has a name for something I already built, and going back. That process is slower and messier than having a supervisor. It also means every correction came from me. I think that matters. And if you find something I missed — I genuinely mean it — I want to know.
